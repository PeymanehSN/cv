I am especially interested in questions where theory alone is not enough: problems that require validation, iteration, and a careful understanding of how people actually respond to incentives, information, and social comparison.

Current research direction

From Dormant Wardrobes to Circular Action

I am developing a theory-guided field experiment on circular-economy decisions, using a digital wardrobe setting to study how behavioral interventions change what people do with unused possessions.

The project asks whether people can be moved from passive ownership to sustainable action when unused items are made visible as economic, social, and environmental assets. It would test how digital valuation, loss framing, peer comparison, default recommendations, marketplace signals, and sustainability feedback affect decisions to sell, donate, reuse, recycle, or keep unused fashion items.

This direction builds on my doctoral research on incentives, wage transparency, envy, peer effects, and organizational design, while extending these mechanisms into sustainable consumption and circular-economy behavior.

BAVO

Digital wardrobe setting for circular-economy behavior

BAVO is a product concept designed to make the hidden economic and sustainability value of wardrobes visible. It can support field experiments on valuation, loss framing, peer comparison, default recommendations, resale, reuse, donation, and recycling decisions.

Behavioral finance and decision-making
